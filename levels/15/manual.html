Level 1 > Lesson 15 > Expresa emociones comunes.

Steps of the method

  • Class routine

    a. Give a brief explanation of the Educational Objective in Spanish.

    Make sure you clearly explain the objective and the skills students are expected to develop in the lesson. This shouldn't take more than a few seconds.

  • Step 1. Go over the warm up activity.

    Teach the sound(s) specified in the lesson.

    1. a. Show the icon of the first sound and say it. Make sure all students are able to see how you articulate the sound.

    2. b. Select a student and ask him/her to say the sound out loud.

    3. c. Spell out the word provided for the sound taught so that the students learn to link the sounds. Select one student and have him/her join the sounds aloud as you gradually show them on the screen. Have the student say the word at least twice. It is not necessary to provide definitions.

    4. d. Continue in the same fashion with the rest of the sounds specified in the lesson.

  • Step 2. Play the video of the lesson.

    Have students watch the video of the new lesson and encourage them to pay close attention so they can visualize the objectives they need to reach.

  • Step 3. Introduce the new vocabulary and/or structures.

    1. a. Select one student and have him/her join the sounds aloud as you gradually show them on the screen. Do not say the sounds. Finally, have the student say the word at least twice.

    2. b. Explain the word using perception and production (using props, images, drawings, and/or acting) to teach vocabulary in a faster and easier way. The way you teach the word is the way you can trigger it later. Make sure the students understand the word.

    3. c. Have one student demonstrate his/her understanding of the new word/expression by either asking the student for an example; using the new word in a sentence; providing a question or indication that guides the student to an example; or involving the student in a short situation where he/she demonstrates the correct usage of the new vocabulary. For example: Watch out for…! You may call on a second student if you consider the word/expression requires further practice for better understanding.

    4. d. Continue in the same fashion with the rest of the words. Make sure you select a different student for each word.

  • Step 4. Play the video again.

    Play the video and encourage students to identify the new words or structures as they are used in the context of the story.

  • Step 5. Repetition

    1. a. Play the first clip of the video.

    2. b. Reproduce the line just played in the clip while acting it out. Use 2 or 3 meaningful gestures per line.

    3. c. Select one student and have him/her meaningfully reproduce the line acting it out. Follow the correction process.

    4. d. Have two more students, one at a time, meaningfully reproduce the line acting it out. Follow the correction process. Make sure students participate evenly throughout the lesson.

    5. e. Continue with each line in the above manner until all the content has been covered.

  • Step 6. Play the video again.

    This is done so that students can organize and integrate all the ideas they have just learned and produced.

  • Step 7. Role-plays

    Have all students participate in role-plays to achieve the objective of the lesson and apply their skills in a real-life context. Select two or three students at a time and have them get involved in either a role-play or a question/answer activity. All students must speak, regardless of how much they say.

    This is your opportunity to get students involved in different scenarios where they will be able to communicate ideas similar to those in the lesson, but using their own personal characteristics.

    Make sure they understand the objective and use the new words and structures learned in the lesson. See the role-plays or activities suggested in the "Role-plays” section for this step as part of your class preparation.

  • Step 8. Review the new vocabulary and structures contained in the lesson.

    Check students’ understanding of all new vocabulary and structures, as well as their pronunciation, by using the same word triggers you employed during the vocabulary introduction. Call on one student at a time to respond aloud with the correct word or expression. If a student is unsure or provides an incorrect response, encourage peer correction by prompting another student to answer, and continue until the correct answer is given. Use the color chart as a reference tool to correct any pronunciation errors effectively.

    This step helps students reinforce criteria and refresh their memory. It is the right moment to reinforce the contents of the lesson with students who had trouble role-playing or asking/answering questions. Props, images, mimics, and drawings may help you, too.

  • Step 9. Practice.

    Each lesson has a different practice activity, and different procedure. Check the corresponding lesson to learn the correct procedure for the activity.

Vocabulary


nervous Similar to anxious, tense. (Act nervous. Bite your fingernails.)
first Number one in a line or a list.
gym class     Physical education class.
but Connector for exception or contrast. She is friendly but very quiet.
also Similar to ‘too’, in addition. Marco is a student. David is also a student.
excited Very happy and with much energy. Today is my birthday. I am very excited.
ready Similar to prepared.
now Similar to ‘at this moment’.
Well EXPRESSION! Exclamation used as an introduction to a conversation.
Let's go!     Expression for invitation. (Informal) (Say the expression and make the gesture for an invitation.)

Practice


This activity will be divided into two parts. First the students will learn vocabulary regarding emotions and then they will practice the emotions in a fun and interactive way.


1. Vocabulary


a. Explain to your students that they will learn vocabulary for emotions.

b. Have a student follow the color chart guide and pronounce the word. Follow the correction process.

c. Demonstrate the emotion and help your students with any doubt they may have.

d. Continue in the same fashion with the rest of the emotions.

Extra activities


If there is any extra time, you may have students engage in further speaking practice related to the contents of the lesson. Considering the students’ level and their ability to communicate ideas, you can have them perform any of the following activities:

  • Challenge

    This activity is intended for the students to practice the elements of the lesson by themselves in a fun way. However, you may go over the activity in class and take advantage to help the students have further practice. The objective is for students to listen to an audio, look at four pictures, and choose the one that matches what they hear.

    Choose a student and play the audio for the first item. Ask the student to answer by saying a, b, c, or d. Then click on the student’s answer. If it is correct, the platform will move to the next item. If it is wrong, the student can keep trying until the correct answer is chosen.

  • Role-play

    This activity helps students to apply what they have learned in real life situations using new vocabulary and structures found in the lesson.

    Select two or three students and assign them a scenario. You may use one of the suggested scenarios from the lesson or create your own, as long as it supports the lesson objective. Have the students perform the role-play, and assist them with any new vocabulary they may need.

    Tell the students to begin a conversation by asking someone about their emotions. Model the following conversation aloud:

    Suggested scenarios:

    1. - In the school corridor

    2. - In front of the university entrance

    3. - On a bench outside the sports facility

    4. - Waiting for the exam to start

    5. - In the lobby, waiting for the karate class

  • Questions and Answers

    In this controlled activity, students can practice asking and answering questions using the target structure.

    Select students in pairs, guiding them to take turns asking and answering questions while keeping the focus on the lesson objective. During the activity, you may provide support with vocabulary, correct errors and use the time to clarify any doubts that arise.

  • Conversation

    This activity helps students expand their vocabulary and see how the lesson concept can be applied to real-life situations. By connecting the topic to their own experiences, students practice using the target language in a more natural and meaningful way.

    Begin with a short conversation on the lesson topic, modeling the target structure. Then, invite students to share their own examples or experiences related to the topic. Encourage them to use the new vocabulary and expressions from the lesson, asking follow-up questions to guide the exchange. Conclude by highlighting useful phrases and addressing any common mistakes.
